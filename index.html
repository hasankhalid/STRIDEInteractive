What are the reasons for low retention and transition rates?

  • Less Post primary schools
  • Long Distances
  • Lack of Transport
  • Local Problems

Less Post primary schools:

Long Distances

Owing to lesser post-primary schools, students have to travel long distances to access middle and secondary level schools.

Lack of Transport

Lack of affordable transportation facilities to access distant middle and secondary schools also discourages retention and transition to post-primary levels.

Local Problems

Transition and retention rates are also direly affected by a host of context specific problems that are only be diagnosed through localized planning and solutions.

How STRIDE addresses these problems?

  • Local Planning
  • Afternoon Schools
  • Transport
  • Community Engagement

Local Planning

STRIDE advocates collecting quantitative and qualitative data on education to guide local planning and policy-making. This approach is guided by the belief that local problems can be best addressed by context specific solutions.

Afternoon Schools

Based on local needs, STRIDE identifies areas with no or less post-primary schools and establishes middle schools in existing primary schools and high schools in existing middle schools. This provides a cost effective solution to increase access to post-primary schools.

Transport

Guided by local planning and in accordance with local needs, STRIDE also provides students with transport facilities and transport vouchers to encourage transition to post-primary schools.

Community Engagement

To ensure successful implementation of STRIDE interventions, I-SAPS also encourages participation and buy in of the local communities in every step.

Supplementing education with work: How Gul Muhammad defied all odds?

Seven years ago, Gul Muhammad attended his last class of grade 8 th in Government Middle School, Ali Kach in district Kohat. Afterwards, he discontinued his education because the nearest high school was more than 10 km away. Taking into consideration his family’s financial conditions, he couldn’t afford to travel such a long distance on a daily basis. Therefore, he decided to help his father with his work and financially support his family. Many children of village Ali Kach drop-out of schools after finishing middle level education due to lack of high schools nearby. Most of them, just like Gul Muhammad, in order to make ends meet tend to help their parents with their work and start supporting their families financially from a young age. Adding to this, those who can and want to go to school tend to drop-out because the timings of their work and school clashes and work is something they cannot afford to compromise on. Before afternoon classes were started in his village, Gul Muhammad longed for going to school and continuing his education. With each passing day, he used to think that his age will inhibit his ability to learn. His mother describes his will to study as, ‘despite having no school nearby, he himself use to take out some time every day and sit in his room for an hour to study.’ As of today, he’s 22 years old, married and also a father of two children. But this hasn’t stopped him from continuing his education. Since the upgradation of the nearest middle school to a high school, he goes to the farm in the morning and goes to school in the afternoon. His grade 9 th final examination results came out a few months ago and, in his own words, he describes that moment as ‘nothing short of a miracle’. Riding on his bicycle provided to him under STRIDE, he is glad that distance is no longer an issue for him. With travel time being saved, he easily manages to work as well as study. If STRIDE wasn’t implemented, his dream to study further would not have become a reality.

Facilitating children with disabilities: The story of Kalsoom Nazir

Suffering from an illness often impedes one’s ability to perform day-to-day tasks. Kalsoom Nazir, residing in village Sard Cheena of district Swabi, is 15 years old and is also a patient of thalassemia. After finishing primary education in March 2017, her fate was hung in a balance since the nearest middle school was at a distance of more than 8 km. Travelling such a distance on foot was neither safe nor her illness allowed her to do so. Further adding to the burden, her parents couldn’t afford a transport facility to ease her travel to and from school. Despite being a constitutional right, education has become out of reach for many. Our current education system hardly caters to the needs of disabled children therefore parents of such children, especially those who cannot bear out-of-pocket spending, decide to discontinue their education. The situation gets more worrisome in rural areas where poverty is rampant and basic facilities are meagre. Kalsoom use to think that after finishing primary education, her days will go by sitting idly at home. However, as of today, she goes to school with ease and also learns adequately. Taking into consideration local needs, afternoon middle classes as per STRIDE were initiated at Government Girls Primary School, Sard Cheena and Kalsoom was one of the first students to have enrolled in this school. Travelling to school is now no longer a problem for her as under STRIDE, a rickshaw picks her up and drops her to school. Her parents are also relieved that they don’t have to worry about her transport costs. “When Kalsoom finished primary, it was quite clear to us that she wouldn’t be able to resume her education. But since STRIDE’s timely implementation in our area, I’m glad that students who were at the risk of being a drop-out have been facilitated to resume their education”, narrates Kalsoom’s mother.
